## Section 1: Basic Legislative Terms (10 questions)

### Question 1
What is the difference between a bill and a Public Act?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **bill** is a proposed law that has not yet been enacted. A **Public Act (PA)** is a bill that has passed both chambers and been signed into law by the Governor. Bills become Public Acts when enacted.
</details>

### Question 2
What does "SB 234" indicate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**SB** means Senate Bill, indicating this bill originated in the Michigan Senate (not the House). The number 234 is the sequential number assigned when introduced.
</details>

### Question 3
What is a sponsor?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The **sponsor** is the Senator who introduces a bill and serves as its primary author and advocate.
</details>

### Question 4
True or False: A resolution has the same force of law as a bill.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**False.** Resolutions are legislative measures that do NOT have the force of law. They are typically ceremonial or procedural. Only bills that become Public Acts are actual laws.
</details>

### Question 5
What does MCL stand for?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Michigan Compiled Law** - the collection of Michigan state statutes already on the books.
</details>

### Question 6
What is the difference between a co-sponsor and a sponsor?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The **sponsor** introduces the bill and is the primary author. **Co-sponsors** are other Senators who add their names in support but did not introduce the bill.
</details>

### Question 7
How many senators are in the Michigan Senate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**38 Senators**
</details>

### Question 8
How long is a Senator's term?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**4 years**
</details>

### Question 9
Who presides over the Michigan Senate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The **Lieutenant Governor** serves as President of the Senate and presides over sessions.
</details>

### Question 10
What is a bicameral legislature?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A legislature with **two chambers** (Senate and House). Michigan has 38 Senators and 110 Representatives.
</details>

## Section 2: Bill Status and Process (10 questions)

### Question 11
What is "First Reading"?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**First Reading** is when a bill is introduced, given a number, and read by title on the Senate floor. The bill is then referred to committee.
</details>

### Question 12
What does "referred to committee" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The bill has been assigned to a committee for review, hearings, and possible amendments before returning to the full Senate.
</details>

### Question 13
What is General Orders?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**General Orders** (also called Committee of the Whole or Second Reading) is the stage where the full Senate debates bills and considers amendments in a less formal setting than Third Reading.
</details>

### Question 14
What is Third Reading?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Third Reading** is the final, formal stage where the Senate votes on passage of a bill. It requires a roll call vote and is subject to the five-day rule.
</details>

### Question 15
What is the five-day rule?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A bill must be available in its final form for **5 calendar days** (not session days) after being placed on Third Reading before the Senate can vote on final passage.
</details>

### Question 16
What does "enrolled" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A bill is **enrolled** when it has passed both chambers in identical form and is being officially prepared to send to the Governor.
</details>

### Question 17
What does it mean when a bill is "tabled"?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Tabled** means postponed indefinitely, which usually kills the bill as it rarely comes back for consideration.
</details>

### Question 18
What is the difference between "postponed" and "tabled"?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Postponed** means delayed to a specific time (the bill will be reconsidered). **Tabled** means postponed indefinitely (bill usually dies).
</details>

### Question 19
If a bill is "defeated," what does that mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The bill did not receive enough votes to pass (fewer than 20 votes in the Senate). The bill is dead unless a motion to reconsider is made.
</details>

### Question 20
What does "vetoed" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The Governor rejected the bill and returned it to the Legislature. The Legislature can attempt to override the veto with a 2/3 vote in both chambers.
</details>

## Section 3: Committee Actions (8 questions)

### Question 21
How many standing committees does the Michigan Senate have?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**17 standing committees** for the 2025-2026 session.
</details>

### Question 22
Which committee reviews all bills with fiscal impact?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The **Appropriations Committee** reviews all bills that spend state money.
</details>

### Question 23
What does "reported with recommendation 'do pass'" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The committee approves the bill **as written** with no changes and sends it to General Orders.
</details>

### Question 24
What does "reported with recommendation 'do pass as amended'" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The committee made **amendments** (changes) and approves the amended version for General Orders.
</details>

### Question 25
What is a committee substitute?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **committee substitute** is when the committee significantly rewrites a bill, replacing the original with a new version.
</details>

### Question 26
If a committee "refers a bill to another committee," what happens?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The bill is sent to a different committee for additional review (common pattern: policy committee â†’ Appropriations).
</details>

### Question 27
What is the most common way bills die?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Dying in committee** through inaction. The committee chair never schedules a hearing, or the committee votes it down. Approximately 60-70% of bills die in committee.
</details>

### Question 28
Who has the most power in a committee?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The **committee chair** because they decide which bills get hearings and control the committee agenda.
</details>

## Section 4: Voting Terms (10 questions)

### Question 29
What are "Yeas" and "Nays"?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Yeas** (or Ayes) are "yes" votes. **Nays** (or Noes) are "no" votes.
</details>

### Question 30
How many votes are needed to pass a bill in the Senate?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**20 votes** (majority of 38 Senators)
</details>

### Question 31
How many votes are needed for immediate effect?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**26 votes** (2/3 of 38 Senators)
</details>

### Question 32
What is a voice vote?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **voice vote** is when Senators collectively say "aye" or "no" without individual votes being recorded. The presiding officer determines the outcome based on volume.
</details>

### Question 33
What is a roll call vote?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **roll call vote** is when each Senator's individual vote is recorded. The Secretary calls each Senator's name and they respond "yes," "no," or "pass."
</details>

### Question 34
What is a quorum?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **quorum** is the minimum number of Senators required to conduct business: **20 of 38** in Michigan Senate.
</details>

### Question 35
What does "Excused" mean in a vote count?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Excused** means a Senator is absent with permission (illness, official duty). They are not counted in the vote total.
</details>

### Question 36
What does "Not Voting" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Not Voting** means a Senator is present but chose not to vote (rare, sometimes due to conflict of interest).
</details>

### Question 37
What is unanimous consent?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Unanimous consent** is when something is done without a formal vote because no Senator objects. Used for routine or non-controversial actions.
</details>

### Question 38
A bill passes 22-16, but immediate effect is 25-13. Is immediate effect granted?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**No, immediate effect is DENIED.** While 25 is more than the 20 needed for passage, immediate effect requires 26 votes. The bill still becomes law but takes effect 90 days after session ends, not immediately.
</details>

## Section 5: Floor Procedures (8 questions)

### Question 39
What does "FA" stand for?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Floor Amendment** - amendments offered by Senators during General Orders (e.g., FA 1, FA 2)
</details>

### Question 40
Can amendments be offered during Third Reading?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Generally **no**. Third Reading is for final passage vote only. Amendments require special consent (unanimous or 2/3 vote), which is rare.
</details>

### Question 41
What is a motion to advance?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **motion to advance to Third Reading** moves a bill from General Orders to the Third Reading calendar after debate and amendments are complete.
</details>

### Question 42
What does "motion to table" do?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Motion to table** postpones action indefinitely, which usually kills the bill.
</details>

### Question 43
What is a point of order?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **point of order** is when a Senator believes Senate rules are being violated and asks the presiding officer to rule on whether the objection is valid.
</details>

### Question 44
What is a motion to reconsider?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **motion to reconsider** brings back a vote that already happened, nullifying the previous vote so a new vote can be taken. Usually must be made the same day or next session day.
</details>

### Question 45
When does the five-day rule start?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
When a bill is **placed on the Third Reading calendar**. The bill must be available for 5 calendar days before final passage vote.
</details>

### Question 46
Are roll call votes required for passage on Third Reading?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Yes.** Third Reading passage votes always require roll call votes (individual votes recorded).
</details>

## Section 6: House Interaction (6 questions)

### Question 47
How many members are in the Michigan House of Representatives?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**110 Representatives**
</details>

### Question 48
What does "concur" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Concur** means one chamber agrees to accept the other chamber's amendments to a bill.
</details>

### Question 49
What does "non-concur" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Non-concur** means one chamber rejects the other chamber's amendments, usually leading to a Conference Committee.
</details>

### Question 50
What is a Conference Committee?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **Conference Committee** is a joint Senate-House committee (3 Senators + 3 Representatives) created to negotiate a compromise when chambers pass different versions of a bill.
</details>

### Question 51
Can legislators amend a Conference Report?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**No.** Conference Reports must be voted up or down as written. No amendments are allowed.
</details>

### Question 52
How many votes are needed for the Senate to concur in House amendments?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**20 votes** (same as for passage - simple majority of 38)
</details>

## Section 7: Special Terms and Situations (8 questions)

### Question 53
What is immediate effect?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Immediate effect** allows a law to take effect immediately upon the Governor's signature instead of waiting 90 days after session ends. Requires 2/3 vote (26 of 38).
</details>

### Question 54
If a bill does NOT have immediate effect, when does it take effect?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**90 days after the Legislature adjourns for the year** (sine die adjournment).
</details>

### Question 55
What does "sine die" mean?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Sine die** (Latin: "without day") means final adjournment of the legislative session. Bills that haven't passed must be reintroduced in the next session.
</details>

### Question 56
What is "lame duck" session?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The period between the November election and the end of the year (sine die), when some legislators who were defeated or are retiring are still serving.
</details>

### Question 57
What is a Conference Report?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The **Conference Report** is the compromise version of a bill produced by a Conference Committee (3+3 members) to resolve differences between Senate and House versions.
</details>

### Question 58
How many votes are needed to override a Governor's veto?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**2/3 vote in both chambers** (26 in Senate, 74 in House).
</details>

### Question 59
What is a pocket veto?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
A **pocket veto** occurs when the Governor takes no action on a bill and the Legislature has adjourned. The bill dies because it cannot be returned to the Legislature.
</details>

### Question 60
Must both chambers pass identical versions of a bill?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
**Yes.** Both the Senate and House must pass **exactly identical versions** before a bill can go to the Governor. This is constitutionally required.
</details>

## Scenario-Based Questions (5 questions)

### Scenario Question 1
You hear: "Senate Bill 345 on General Orders. Senator Lee offers Floor Amendment 1. Voice vote. Amendment adopted. Senator Smith moves to advance to Third Reading. Motion carried."

What happened?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
- SB 345 was on General Orders
- FA 1 offered by Sen. Lee: ADOPTED (voice vote)
- Motion to advance to Third Reading: CARRIED
- Bill is now on Third Reading calendar (five-day rule begins)
</details>

### Scenario Question 2
You hear: "Third Reading of Senate Bill 456. Roll call: Yeas 19, Nays 19. The bill fails."

Why did it fail?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The bill needed **20 votes** (majority of 38) to pass. It received only 19 votes, so it **failed**. (Note: If it were truly 19-19, the Lt. Governor could break the tie, but with 38 total, ties are actually rare. This would mean senators absent or not voting.)
</details>

### Scenario Question 3
"Message from House: House passed Senate Bill 567 with amendments. Senator Martinez moves to concur. Roll call: Yeas 21, Nays 17. What happens next?"

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The Senate **concurred** in the House amendments (21 votes, needs 20). Since both chambers have now passed identical versions, the bill is **enrolled and sent to the Governor**.
</details>

### Scenario Question 4
"Senate Bill 789 passes 30-8. Immediate effect vote: 25-13. What is the status?"

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
- Bill **PASSED** (30 votes, needs 20) âœ“
- Immediate effect **DENIED** (25 votes, needs 26) âœ—
- Bill still becomes law but takes effect **90 days after sine die** (not immediately)
- Bill sent to House for House process
</details>

### Scenario Question 5
"The Committee on Education reports SB 234 with recommendation 'do pass as amended' and refers the bill to the Committee on Appropriations. Where does the bill go?"

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The bill goes to the **Appropriations Committee** (NOT to General Orders). Multiple referrals mean the bill goes to the next committee first. Only after Appropriations reports the bill will it go to General Orders.
</details>
