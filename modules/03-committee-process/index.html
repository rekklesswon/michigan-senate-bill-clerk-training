    Module 3: Committee Process and Structure

    Progress

    Module 3 of 6 2-3 hours

    Estimated Time: 2-3 hours

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this module, you will be able to: - List all 17 Michigan Senate standing committees and their jurisdictions - Describe the roles of committee chairs, vice chairs, and members - Explain committee procedures including hearings, votes, and reports - Code committee actions accurately as a Bill Clerk - Understand special situations like discharge motions and multiple referrals - Recognize the special role of the Appropriations Committee

    Why Committees Matter

    Committees are where the real work of the Legislature happens. They: - Screen bills: Most bills die in committee (60-70%) - Hold hearings: Gather expert testimony and public input - Amend bills: Make technical and policy changes - Conduct oversight: Review state agencies and programs - Develop expertise: Members specialize in policy areas

    For Bill Clerks: You must accurately code which committee has a bill, what action the committee took, and when bills are reported back to the full Senate.

    The 17 Michigan Senate Standing Committees (2025-2026 Session)

    1. Appropriations Committee

    Jurisdiction: State budget, funding for all state operations and programs

    Why Special: - Controls the purse strings - All bills with fiscal impact (spending state money) eventually go here - Often the last committee to see a bill before floor action - Largest committee with most members - Has subcommittees for different budget areas (education, health, transportation, etc.) - Committee of jurisdiction for budget bills

    Typical Path: Policy committee Appropriations Floor

    Example Bills: - State budget bills - K-12 school aid budget - Bills creating new state programs (after policy committee approves policy) - Bills expanding existing programs

    Bill Clerk Note: If you hear "referred to Appropriations," the bill involves spending money.

    2. Civil Rights, Judiciary, and Public Safety Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Court system and judicial procedures - Criminal law and sentencing - Civil rights and discrimination law - Prisons and corrections - Law enforcement and police - Domestic violence - Legal system reform

    Example Bills: - Sentencing reform - Expungement of criminal records - Police accountability measures - Civil rights protections - Court procedures and rules

    3. Economic and Community Development Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Economic development initiatives - Small business support - Tourism promotion - Community revitalization - Economic incentives - Regional economic planning - Entrepreneurship

    Example Bills: - Business tax incentives - Downtown redevelopment programs - Tourism marketing budgets - Small business loan programs

    4. Education Committee

    Jurisdiction: - K-12 education policy (not budget - that's Appropriations) - School curriculum and standards - Teacher certification and evaluation - School choice and charter schools - Education accountability - School safety - Special education

    Example Bills: - Graduation requirements - Teacher certification rules - School choice expansion - Curriculum standards (reading, math, etc.) - School disciplinary policies

    Note: Education budget goes to Appropriations; education policy comes here first.

    5. Elections and Ethics Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Election law and procedures - Voter registration - Campaign finance - Legislative ethics - Redistricting - Ballot initiatives - Absentee voting rules

    Example Bills: - Voter ID requirements - Campaign contribution limits - Ethics rules for legislators - Election security measures - Absentee ballot procedures

    6. Energy and Environment Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Energy policy and regulation - Utilities (electric, gas, water) - Environmental protection - Climate policy - Renewable energy - Oil and gas regulation - Air and water quality

    Example Bills: - Renewable energy standards - Utility rate regulation - Environmental cleanup programs - Energy efficiency standards - Pipeline safety

    7. Finance, Insurance, and Consumer Protection Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Banking and financial institutions - Insurance regulation - Consumer protection laws - Financial services regulation - Credit and lending - Debt collection - Identity theft

    Example Bills: - Insurance rate regulations - Consumer fraud prevention - Banking regulations - Cryptocurrency regulation - Predatory lending protections

    8. Government Operations Committee

    Jurisdiction: - State government structure and organization - Civil service and state employees - Administrative procedures - Open records and transparency - FOIA (Freedom of Information Act) - State contracts and procurement - Government efficiency

    Example Bills: - State agency reorganization - Civil service reforms - FOIA expansions or exemptions - State purchasing rules - Government transparency measures

    9. Health Policy Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Healthcare policy and regulation - Medicaid (but not budget) - Public health - Health insurance - Hospitals and medical facilities - Mental health services - Prescription drugs - Healthcare workforce

    Example Bills: - Medicaid expansion or restrictions - Hospital regulations - Mental health parity - Prescription drug pricing - Telehealth rules - Healthcare provider licensing

    10. Housing and Human Services Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Affordable housing - Homeless services - Welfare programs (TANF, food assistance) - Child welfare and foster care - Family services - Adoption - Senior services

    Example Bills: - Affordable housing incentives - Foster care reforms - Welfare eligibility and benefits - Homeless assistance programs - Senior citizen services

    11. Labor Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Employment law - Workers' compensation - Unemployment insurance - Workplace safety (OSHA) - Labor unions and collective bargaining - Minimum wage - Right-to-work laws - Workplace discrimination

    Example Bills: - Minimum wage adjustments - Paid leave policies - Workers' compensation reforms - Workplace safety standards - Union organizing rules

    12. Local Government Committee

    Jurisdiction: - County, city, township government - Local taxation and finance - Municipal services - Intergovernmental relations - Local government authority - Annexation and incorporation - Local revenue sharing

    Example Bills: - Local tax authority - County governance structures - Revenue sharing formulas - Local government consolidation - Municipal service delivery

    13. Natural Resources and Agriculture Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Farming and agriculture - Forestry - Wildlife and hunting/fishing - State parks and recreation - Department of Natural Resources oversight - Agricultural economics - Conservation - Land use

    Example Bills: - Hunting and fishing regulations - Agricultural subsidies or regulations - Forestry management - State park improvements - Conservation easements - Farm labor issues

    14. Oversight Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Investigation of state agencies - Government accountability - Waste, fraud, and abuse - Program effectiveness - Agency audits - Regulatory review

    Special Purpose: - Investigative committee - Can subpoena witnesses and documents - Often deals with controversies and scandals - May not handle many regular bills

    Example Activities: - Investigating agency mismanagement - Reviewing state contracts for fraud - Holding hearings on program failures

    15. Regulatory Affairs Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Professional licensing (doctors, lawyers, plumbers, etc.) - Occupational regulations - Professional boards - Business licensing - Regulatory reform - Administrative rules review

    Example Bills: - Licensing requirements for professions - Occupational regulation reforms - Professional board authority - Regulatory barriers to entry

    16. Transportation and Infrastructure Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Roads and bridges - Public transportation - Transportation funding - Road commissions - Airports - Railroads - Infrastructure investment - Vehicle regulations (some)

    Example Bills: - Gas tax increases - Road funding formulas - Public transit funding - Transportation infrastructure bonds - Road construction standards

    17. Veterans and Emergency Services Committee

    Jurisdiction: - Veterans' benefits and services - Military affairs - Emergency management - Homeland security - Disaster response - State military facilities - Veterans' homes

    Example Bills: - Veterans' benefits - Military base support - Emergency preparedness - Veterans' healthcare access - Disaster relief programs

    Committee Roles and Structure

    Committee Chair

    Appointed by: Majority Leader

    Powers: - Sets committee agenda - Decides which bills get hearings - Chairs committee meetings - Controls debate and recognition - Most important power: Can kill bills by never scheduling hearings - From majority party

    Influence: The chair controls the committee. If chair opposes a bill, it rarely moves forward.

    Majority Vice Chair

    Appointed by: Majority Leader

    Role: - From majority party - Assists chair - Presides when chair is absent - May have special responsibilities (e.g., budget subcommittees)

    Minority Vice Chair

    Appointed by: Majority Leader

    Role: - From minority party - Represents minority party interests on committee - Presides when both chair and majority vice chair are absent - Provides minority perspective in committee deliberations

    Committee Members

    • Attend hearings and meetings
    • Question witnesses
    • Offer amendments
    • Vote on bills
    • Typically 5-15 members per committee
    • Party ratio usually reflects Senate overall (e.g., if Senate is 20-18, committee might be 5-4)

    Committee Staff

    • Not senators: Professional staff hired by committee
    • Research issues
    • Draft amendments
    • Brief senators
    • Manage logistics of hearings

    Bill Clerk Note: You don't code staff actions, only senator actions.

    Committee Procedures

    How Committee Meetings Work

    1. Scheduling

    • Chair decides when to meet
    • Meetings publicly noticed (usually 5 days advance)
    • Held in Capitol or other state buildings

    2. Public Hearings

    • Allow citizens, experts, lobbyists to testify
    • Witnesses register in advance or day-of
    • Typical order: Bill sponsor, proponents, opponents, neutral parties, public comment
    • Committee members ask questions

    3. Committee Discussion

    • Senators debate bill among themselves
    • May request testimony from state agencies
    • Staff provide analysis

    4. Amendments

    • Committee members offer amendments
    • Committee votes on amendments
    • Amendments can make small or large changes

    5. Committee Vote

    • Chair calls for vote on bill
    • Voice vote or roll call
    • Majority of committee members present must vote yes to report bill

    6. Committee Report

    • If bill passes committee, it's "reported" back to full Senate
    • Report includes committee recommendation (see next section)

    Committee Actions Bill Clerks Must Code

    When a committee acts on a bill, Bill Clerks must accurately code the action. Here are the possible outcomes:

    1. Reported with Recommendation "Do Pass"

    What it means: Committee approves bill as written, no changes

    Bill Clerk Code: "Committee reports SB X with recommendation 'do pass'"

    What happens next: Bill goes to General Orders (Committee of the Whole)

    Example: "The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure reports Senate Bill 145 with the recommendation 'do pass.'"

    2. Reported with Recommendation "Do Pass as Amended"

    What it means: Committee made changes (amendments) and approves amended version

    Bill Clerk Code: "Committee reports SB X with recommendation 'do pass as amended'"

    What happens next: Bill goes to General Orders with committee amendments

    Example: "The Committee on Health Policy reports Senate Bill 212 with the recommendation 'do pass as amended.'"

    Note: The amendments become part of the bill unless General Orders rejects them.

    3. Reported with Committee Substitute (CS)

    What it means: Committee rewrote significant portions; original bill replaced with substitute

    Bill Clerk Code: "Committee reports SB X with substitute (committee substitute)"

    What happens next: Substitute goes to General Orders (original version is gone)

    Example: "The Committee on Education reports Senate Bill 78 with a committee substitute."

    Note: Substitutes are major rewrites. Original sponsor may not even recognize the bill.

    4. Referred to Another Committee

    What it means: Committee sends bill to different committee for review

    Bill Clerk Code: "Committee refers SB X to [Committee Name]"

    What happens next: Bill goes to the other committee (starts committee process over)

    Common scenarios: - Policy committee Appropriations (bill has fiscal impact) - One policy committee Different policy committee (jurisdictional issues) - Substantive committee Oversight (investigation needed)

    Example: "The Committee on Natural Resources and Agriculture reports Senate Bill 198 and refers the bill to the Committee on Appropriations."

    Note: This is NOT a negative action. The first committee may have approved it but recognized fiscal impact.

    5. Postponed

    What it means: Committee delays decision, may reconsider later

    Bill Clerk Code: "Committee postponed SB X"

    What happens next: Bill stays in committee, may come back up for vote (or may never be seen again)

    Reasons for postponement: - Need more information - Waiting for fiscal analysis - Political timing issues - Sponsor requested delay to work on amendments - Close vote, chair wants to delay until more members present

    Example: "The Committee on Labor postponed consideration of Senate Bill 334."

    6. No Action / No Report

    What it means: Committee never votes on bill, or votes it down

    Bill Clerk Code: Usually nothing to code (absence of action)

    What happens next: Bill dies in committee

    Most common outcome: Chair never schedules hearing, or committee votes "no" on reporting bill

    Note: If committee votes down a bill, you might hear "motion to report failed" but often there's just silence - bill never emerges from committee.

    7. Discharged from Committee (Rare)

    What it means: Full Senate votes to take bill away from committee

    Bill Clerk Code: "Senate discharged [Committee] from further consideration of SB X"

    What happens next: Bill goes to floor without committee recommendation

    How it happens: - Requires motion and majority vote on Senate floor - Used when committee chair is blocking popular bill - Rare because it's seen as insulting to committee and chair - Usually requires bipartisan support

    Example: "Senator Martinez moved to discharge the Committee on Elections and Ethics from further consideration of Senate Bill 56. Motion carried, Yeas 22, Nays 16."

    Special Committee Situations

    Multiple Referrals (Sequential)

    What it is: Bill goes to one committee, then another, in sequence

    Common pattern: Policy committee Appropriations

    Example: "The Committee on Health Policy reports Senate Bill 401 with the recommendation 'do pass as amended' and refers the bill to the Committee on Appropriations."

    Bill Clerk Action: Code both the committee recommendation AND the referral to next committee

    Why it happens: Bill has both policy implications (first committee) and fiscal impact (Appropriations)

    Subcommittees

    What they are: Smaller groups within a committee that focus on specific areas

    Most common in: Appropriations Committee - Appropriations has subcommittees for: Education, Health, Transportation, etc. - Each subcommittee handles budget bills for its area

    Process: 1. Bill referred to full Appropriations Committee 2. Chair assigns to subcommittee 3. Subcommittee holds hearing and votes 4. Subcommittee reports to full committee 5. Full committee votes 6. Full committee reports to Senate floor

    Bill Clerk Action: Code subcommittee reports separately from full committee reports

    Example: "The Appropriations Subcommittee on Education reports Senate Bill 501 to the full Appropriations Committee with recommendation 'do pass.'"

    Joint Committee Hearings

    What they are: Senate and House committees meet together to hear testimony

    Why: Save time when both chambers considering similar bills

    Bill Clerk Note: Each chamber's committee still votes separately and reports separately

    Committee of the Whole (Not a Standing Committee!)

    What it is: The entire Senate acting as one committee (General Orders stage)

    Not a standing committee: It's a procedural stage (covered in Module 4)

    Difference: - Standing committees: 5-15 senators, closed process - Committee of the Whole: All 38 senators, floor debate

    The Appropriations Committee: A Special Case

    The Appropriations Committee deserves extra attention because it's unique:

    Why Appropriations is Different:

    1. All spending bills go here eventually
    2. Even if bill starts in another committee

    3. Appropriations has "last look" at fiscal impact

    4. Largest committee

    5. More members than other committees

    6. Divided into subcommittees

    7. Budget bills originate here

    8. State budget starts in Appropriations

    9. Education budget, department budgets, etc.

    10. More staff support

    11. Fiscal analysts

    12. Budget experts

    13. Special power

    14. Can effectively kill bills by declaring them "too expensive"
    15. Can demand offsets (spending cuts elsewhere to pay for new spending)

    Fiscal Notes

    • All bills with spending get a "fiscal note" (cost estimate)
    • Prepared by nonpartisan analysts
    • Appropriations Committee reviews fiscal notes
    • Committee may amend bill to reduce costs

    Bill Clerk Note: When you see a bill referred to Appropriations, you know it spends money.

    Public Involvement in Committee Hearings

    Who Can Testify:

    • Bill sponsors (always go first)
    • Citizens
    • Interest groups and lobbyists
    • Experts (academics, researchers)
    • State agency officials
    • Local government representatives

    How Public Hearings Work:

    1. Committee chair opens hearing
    2. Bill sponsor presents bill
    3. Proponents testify
    4. Opponents testify
    5. Neutral parties testify
    6. Public comment (if time allows)
    7. Committee members ask questions
    8. Chair closes hearing

    Important for Bill Clerks:

    • You don't code public testimony
    • You only code committee actions (votes, reports, referrals)
    • Hearings can last hours, but the vote takes seconds

    Self-Check Quiz

    Question 1: List 10 of the 17 Michigan Senate standing committees. **Answer:** (Any 10 of these) 1. Appropriations 2. Civil Rights, Judiciary, and Public Safety 3. Economic and Community Development 4. Education 5. Elections and Ethics 6. Energy and Environment 7. Finance, Insurance, and Consumer Protection 8. Government Operations 9. Health Policy 10. Housing and Human Services 11. Labor 12. Local Government 13. Natural Resources and Agriculture 14. Oversight 15. Regulatory Affairs 16. Transportation and Infrastructure 17. Veterans and Emergency Services
    Question 2: Which committee handles all bills with fiscal impact? **Answer:** The Appropriations Committee. Bills with spending often go to a policy committee first, then are referred to Appropriations for fiscal review.
    Question 3: What is the difference between "reported with recommendation do pass" and "reported with recommendation do pass as amended"? **Answer:** - **"Do pass"** means the committee approves the bill as written with no changes - **"Do pass as amended"** means the committee made amendments (changes) and approves the amended version
    Question 4: Who has the most power in a committee and why? **Answer:** The committee chair has the most power because the chair decides which bills get hearings. If the chair doesn't schedule a hearing, the bill dies in committee. The chair also sets the agenda, controls debate, and manages committee operations.
    Question 5: A bill about K-12 teacher certification is referred to committee. Which committee would it likely go to? **Answer:** The Education Committee (deals with K-12 policy, including teacher certification, curriculum, and school operations).
    Question 6: What does it mean when a committee "refers a bill to another committee"? **Answer:** The bill is sent to a different committee for additional review. This is common when a bill has both policy implications and fiscal impact (policy committee Appropriations), or when jurisdiction is unclear. The bill starts the committee process over in the new committee.
    Question 7: What is a committee substitute? **Answer:** A committee substitute is when the committee significantly rewrites a bill. The original bill is replaced with the substitute version, which may be substantially different from what the sponsor introduced.
    Question 8: True or False: If a committee postpones a bill, the bill automatically dies. **Answer:** False. Postponement means the committee delays its decision. The bill may come back up for a vote later (though it often doesn't). The bill only dies if the committee never acts on it or votes it down.
    Question 9: What is a discharge motion and when is it used? **Answer:** A discharge motion is when the full Senate votes to remove a bill from committee and bring it to the floor. It's used (rarely) when a committee chair is blocking a bill that has broader support. Requires a majority vote on the Senate floor and is considered an extraordinary measure.
    Question 10: A committee reports a bill "do pass" and refers it to Appropriations. What should a Bill Clerk code? **Answer:** Code both actions: (1) The committee recommendation "do pass" and (2) the referral to the Appropriations Committee. The bill now goes to Appropriations, not to General Orders.
    Question 11: What committee would handle a bill about: (a) Medicaid expansion, (b) minimum wage increase, (c) hunting regulations? **Answer:** - (a) **Health Policy** (Medicaid policy) - then likely Appropriations (fiscal impact) - (b) **Labor** (employment law, wages) - (c) **Natural Resources and Agriculture** (wildlife, hunting/fishing regulations)
    Question 12: What is the most common way bills die? **Answer:** Dying in committee through inaction. The committee chair never schedules a hearing, or the committee votes not to report the bill. Approximately 60-70% of bills die in committee.

    Practice Scenario: Coding Committee Actions

    You're a Bill Clerk listening to committee reports. Code each action:

    Scenario 1: "The Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure reports Senate Bill 789 with the recommendation 'do pass.'"

    What do you code? **Answer:** Committee action: Reported with recommendation "do pass" Committee: Transportation and Infrastructure Bill: SB 789 Next stage: Bill goes to General Orders

    Scenario 2: "The Committee on Education reports Senate Bill 234 with the recommendation 'do pass as amended' and refers the bill to the Committee on Appropriations."

    What do you code? **Answer:** Committee action: Reported "do pass as amended" + referred to Appropriations Committee: Education Bill: SB 234 Next committee: Appropriations Note: Bill does NOT go to General Orders yet; goes to Appropriations first

    Scenario 3: "The Committee on Health Policy reports Senate Bill 567 with a committee substitute."

    What do you code? **Answer:** Committee action: Reported with committee substitute Committee: Health Policy Bill: SB 567 Next stage: Committee substitute version goes to General Orders Note: Original bill replaced with substitute

    Scenario 4: "The Committee on Appropriations postponed consideration of Senate Bill 445."

    What do you code? **Answer:** Committee action: Postponed Committee: Appropriations Bill: SB 445 Next stage: Bill stays in committee; may be reconsidered later (or may die)

    Notes for Instructor Q&A

    Q: How do I know which committee a bill should go to if the subject overlaps multiple committees?
    A: Senate leadership (primarily the Majority Leader) makes the final decision. Some bills could go to multiple committees. Listen for the referral announcement. Don't guess - wait for the official referral.

    Q: Can a bill skip committee?
    A: Extremely rare. Theoretically possible with a discharge motion, but virtually all bills go through committee. The committee process is considered essential.

    Q: What if I miss which committee a bill was referred to?
    A: Check the Senate Journal (official record) or ask your supervisor. Never guess on committee referrals.

    Q: Do I need to know every committee member's name?
    A: Not initially, but you'll learn the committee chairs quickly. Focus on knowing the 17 committees and their jurisdictions first.

    Q: What if a committee reports a bill and I'm not sure if it's "do pass" or "do pass as amended"?
    A: Listen carefully for "as amended." If you're unsure, flag it and ask your supervisor. Accuracy is critical.

    Key Takeaways

    17 standing committees handle different policy areas
    Appropriations Committee reviews all bills with fiscal impact
    Committee chair controls whether bills get hearings
    Most bills die in committee (60-70%)
    Committee actions: do pass, do pass as amended, substitute, refer, postpone, no action
    Bills often go to policy committee first, then Appropriations
    Bill Clerks must accurately code all committee actions
